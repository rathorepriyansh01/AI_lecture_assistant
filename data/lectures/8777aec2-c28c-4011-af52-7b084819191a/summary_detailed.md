The lecture provided an introductory overview of healthcare, tracing its evolution from ancient practices to modern high-tech care. It defined health by the WHO standard and highlighted the dramatic global increase in life expectancy since 1800, noting societal impacts and narrowing disparities. It then detailed population health trends, including age-specific mortality patterns, persistent US health disparities, and the shift from infectious to chronic diseases (like heart disease and cancer) as leading causes of death.

The speaker distinguished between mortality, morbidity, and disability, introducing "quality of life" metrics (ADLs/IADLs). They proposed two strategies for extending healthy lifespan: "compression of morbidity" (long healthy life, rapid decline) and "lifespan extension" (proportional reduction in all decline stages).

The lecture emphasized that medical care is a crucial cyclic process of diagnosis, prognosis, and therapy, where patient response continuously informs revisions, contrasting this with failed "one-shot" AI approaches. This led to the concept of a "learning healthcare system" that continuously observes, analyzes, and improves care, moving beyond the limitations of traditional randomized controlled trials (RCTs) in drug development.

Public health's historical role in data collection and disease classification (e.g., ICD, John Snow's epidemiology) was discussed. Finally, the lecture addressed significant public health challenges: the controversial nature of quarantine and the escalating, inefficient cost of healthcare, particularly in the US, attributing it to low productivity growth and insufficient IT investment. It concluded by stressing the critical role of medical informaticians in bridging this technological gap.
