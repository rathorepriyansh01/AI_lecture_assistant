This lecture provides a comprehensive overview of medicine, tracing its evolution from ancient shamanism to modern high-tech care, while highlighting the enduring importance of the healer-patient relationship. It defines health broadly and examines key metrics like life expectancy, noting dramatic global improvements alongside persistent disparities and the historical shift from infectious to chronic diseases as leading causes of death. The lecture explores concepts of quality of life, morbidity, and disability, advocating for "compression of morbidity" over mere lifespan extension.

It then outlines the core tasks of healthcare (diagnosis, prognosis, therapy) as a crucial **cyclic process** of continuous data gathering, hypothesis testing, and treatment revision, a concept often missed by early AI. This iterative approach underpins the vision of a "Learning Health Care System" (LHS), which aims to generate knowledge from ongoing patient care, contrasting with the limitations of traditional randomized clinical trials.

The lecture delves into public health's role, detailing the history of data collection (e.g., London Bills of Mortality, ICD classification) and pioneering epidemiological work like John Snow's cholera investigation. It discusses public health measures such as quarantines, acknowledging their ethical complexities.

Finally, it addresses the exceptionally high cost of healthcare, particularly in the US, attributing it to insatiable demand, technological advancements, and low productivity growth compared to other sectors. The lecture concludes by emphasizing the critical need for greater IT investment and the development of medical informaticians to improve efficiency and foster a truly learning healthcare system.
