## Introduction to Risk-Derivation for Type 2 Diabetes
The lecture section focuses on the risk-derivation for type 2 diabetes, with a specific goal in mind. 

### Goal of Risk-Derivation
The primary objective is to address the significant number of undiagnosed cases of type 2 diabetes in the United States. 
* The goal is based on the fact that a substantial portion of the population is affected by undiagnosed type 2 diabetes.
* Specifically, it is noted that 25% of people in the United States have undiagnosed type 2 diabetes.

### Understanding Type 2 Diabetes
Type 2 diabetes is a medical condition that affects the way the body processes glucose. 
* **Definition:** Type 2 diabetes is characterized by insulin resistance, where the body's cells do not respond effectively to insulin, and impaired insulin secretion.
* **Importance of Diagnosis:** Early diagnosis and treatment of type 2 diabetes are crucial to prevent long-term complications and improve health outcomes.

### Significance of Risk-Derivation
Risk-derivation for type 2 diabetes is essential to identify individuals at high risk of developing the condition. 
* **Purpose:** The purpose of risk-derivation is to enable early intervention and prevention strategies, reducing the likelihood of developing type 2 diabetes.
* **Examples:** Risk-derivation models can be used to identify high-risk individuals, such as those with a family history of type 2 diabetes, obesity, or physical inactivity.

### Key Statistics and Implications
The prevalence of undiagnosed type 2 diabetes in the United States is a significant concern. 
* **Prevalence:** 25% of people in the United States have undiagnosed type 2 diabetes.
* **Implications:** This statistic highlights the need for effective risk-derivation and screening strategies to identify and diagnose type 2 diabetes early.
